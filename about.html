Our Story

From a conversation in Detroit to a global health problem

BlueBil began not in a lab, but in a conversation. After speaking with neonatologists at Henry Ford St. John Hospital in Detroit and clinicians at Komfo Anokye Teaching Hospital in Kumasi, Ghana, the same frustration emerged from both sides of the world: existing non-invasive bilirubin tools were failing darker-skinned babies — and no one had fixed it.

We believed the solution was hiding in plain sight. The sclera — the white of the eye — yellows with bilirubin just like the skin, but without melanin interference. The science was there. The device wasn't. So we built it.

2024

Stakeholder Discovery

Clinical conversations at Henry Ford St. John (Detroit) and Komfo Anokye Teaching Hospital (Ghana) surface the equity gap in neonatal bilirubin diagnostics.

Late 2024

Proof of Concept — Glasses Platform

First prototype fabricated using SLA-printed glasses frame with embedded blue LED, IR LED, and matched phototransistors. Core scleral sensing principle validated with first calibration dataset.

2025

Pistol-Grip Prototype

Transitioned to handheld pistol-grip enclosure with three-wavelength architecture, rubber barrel guard, and full ESP32 firmware. Five phantom concentrations validated: R² = 0.976.

Early 2026

Rice360 Design Competition

BlueBil presented at the Rice360 Institute for Global Health Technologies 16th Annual Design Competition. University of Michigan Innovation Partnerships engaged for patentability assessment.

Next

Clinical Pilot Studies

Enhanced scattering phantoms, dual-wavelength model refinement, and partnership with domestic and international NICU programs for paired TSB-TsB validation across a diverse neonatal population.

Global Impact

A diagnostic gap with global consequences

Severe neonatal jaundice is nearly 100 times more prevalent in low- and middle-income countries, where it ranks as the 7th and 8th leading cause of neonatal death in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia respectively. Laboratory infrastructure, trained personnel, and consumable supplies needed for serum bilirubin testing are frequently unavailable.

Many births occur in rural clinics where laboratory turnaround times may be delayed by hours to days, preventing timely intervention during the narrow therapeutic window in which severe hyperbilirubinemia can be safely treated.

BlueBil's reusable, consumable-free design and infrastructure-independent operation directly address these constraints. Our tiered pricing model targets sub-$400 LMIC deployment through institutional procurement, NGO-subsidized distribution, and public health purchasing programs.
